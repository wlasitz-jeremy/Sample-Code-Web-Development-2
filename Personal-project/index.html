Scuba Diving

*Being a Diver*

-Being a Diver 1-

Depth and Pressure

Underwater, you're under more pressure because water also has weight, which combines with the atmosphere's weight(pressure). Since water has more density and is heavier than air 10 meters/33 feet exerts the same pressure as the whole atmosphere. Therefore, the pressure increases by one bar/ata for each 10 meters /33 feet you descend (go down). Likewise, the pressure decreases by one bar/ata for every 10 meters/33 feet you ascend (go up).

Pressure, Air Volume and Density

Water can not be compressed so its volume and density do not change with pressure changes. Since your body tissues are mostly made of water, you do not feel the pressure changes on most of your body while diving. Pressure changes do change the volume and density of air (or any other gas). As the pressure increases - as you go deeper - a gas volume decreases because the gas molecules get compressed. The gas density increases because all the molecules are there, but they're packed into a smaller area. This affects all the air spaces in, or in contact with, your body. These include your ears, sinuses, lungs, mask, and when using one, a dry suit. You'll also learn that this principal affects controlling your buoyancy, how long your air supply lasts, and some important safety rules. Air volume and density change proportionately with pressure. This means if you go from the surface to 10 meters/33 feet, you double the pressure to 2 bar/ata(1 air plus 1 water), a given air volume halves, and its density doubles. If you go to 20 meters/66 feet, the pressure is 3 bar/ata(1 air plus 2 water). An air volume would be one-third the surface volume, and density would be triple.

-Being a Diver 2-

Seeing and Hearing as a Diver

Let's investigate how being underwater affects how you see and hear.

Apparent size and Distance

Water is about 800 times denser than air, which causes many of the differences between the terrestrial and aquatic worlds. One effect of this is that it causes light to change direction slightly (refract) when it transfers from water into the air in your mask. This direction change magnifies objects by about a third. Depending on your perspective objects look larger, closer, or both than they really are. you may reach for something and miss because it's farther away than it appears. With experience, you learn to compensate unconsciously.

Color Loss and Scattering

Water also affects light by reflecting, scattering and absorbing it. This means that as you go deeper, it gets darker. During the day, at the most popular dive sites you usually have adequate light. On most dives, it does not seem dark at all, though on others, like night dives or when the visibility is poor, it obviously does. In these conditions, you can use a dive light - you'll get to do this during the Night Adventure Dive in the PADI Open Water Diver Course. Water also absorbs light directly, but not uniformly. As light travels through it, water absorbs some colors before others. The farther light travels through water, the more color it loses. As you go deeper, red is the first color absorbed, followed by orange, yellow, green, and finally blue. You see the same color lose when looking at distant objects. Distant objects tend to become more colorful as you get closer. Your eyes adjust somewhat for the color change, but shinning a light at depth brings back lost color and shows the difference.

Hearing Underwater

Water affects what you hear underwater, because it transmits sound more efficiently than does air. You will hear many new sounds from aquatic life, boats, divers, and other sources. Underwater sound travels over long distances. For example, a boat that you can not hear at the surface may sound relatively close underwater. Sound travels about four times faster in water than in air. This makes it difficult to determine its source - underwater, it usually seems like sound is coming form all around, or directly overhead. You may be able to tell relative change in distance by a sound growing louder or fainter, even though you can not tell the direction.

-Being a Diver 3-

Dive environments and Conditions

Part of dive planning includes recognizing what to expect in different dive environments and how conditions can affect dive plans.

Types of Dive Environments

The reason diving is so popular all over the world is because you can enjoy it in many different environments, each with their own characteristics. Common popular environments include:

  • coral reefs
  • lakes and reservoirs
  • rivers
  • springs
  • flooded quarries

In these environments, six general conditions can affect you. The degree to which condition varies and affects you depends on the environment. Some environments have little temperature change, whereas others have significant changes. Some places never have water movement, others always do and yet others may or may not, depending upon different factors. The six general conditions are:

  • temperature
  • visibility
  • water movement
  • bottom composition
  • aquatic life
  • sunlight

Temperature

Diving takes place in water as cool as near freezing(-2 degrees Celsius/28 Fahrenheit) to (30 degree Celsius/85 Fahrenheit). Most diving takes place in water on the warm end of this range, but thanks to modern exposure suits, often in water cooler than we would otherwise be comfortable.

Visibility

Visibility underwater ranges from 0 meters to more than 60 meters/200 feet and influences your dive significantly. You want to learn how to adjust your dive based to visibility, how to keep from reducing your visibility by your actions and when visibility is too poor to dive. You measure visibility as the distance you can see horizontally underwater. This can be subjective - you may be able recognize the silhouette of a diver 12 meters/40 feet away, and some would call this 12 meter/40 foot visibility. Others would use a shorter distance at which they can make out the details.

Water Movement

Two types of water motion primarily affect you while diving: waves and currents. Waves result primarily from wind blowing over the waters surface. The stronger the wind and the larger the distance it passes over, the larger the wave can grow. Waves affect swimming at the surface, as well as the techniques you use entering and exiting the water. Currents result from waves, water heating and cooling, the earth's rotation and tides. Those caused by waves vary with wind direction. Due to water rising or sinking as it heats or cools, respectively, currents are created that tend to be seasonal. Although such water motion is initially vertical, when it reaches the bottom or an obstacle, the flow can continue horizontally. Major oceanic currents and counter-currents result from earth's rotation. Although these permanent currents, their paths and strength vary to a degree due to other influences. Tides, which result from the gravitational pull of the sun and moon, lift the oceans and cause water levels to rise and fall as the earth rotates.

Bottom Composition

You can divide the different bottom composition into five basic types:

  • Silt/Mud - Clay, fine organic/inorganic material or mud
  • Sand - Larger, coarser particles
  • Rock - Gravel and large rock
  • Coral - Living and dead coral in tropical water
  • Vegetation - Various plants and algae found in both fresh and salt water

Contact tends to reduce visibility. So, avoiding contact keeps the water clearer. In environments like coral or rock, you can get cuts or scrapes if you're not careful. Bottom contact also has risks of stings or punctures from organisms like hydroids or sea urchins. Vegetation bottoms like kelp might have some entanglement risks. Aquatic life on the bottom is fragile and sensitive. Resting or kneeling on them, or kicking them with your fin tips, can injure or destroy them. Staying off the bottom helps preserve the site's natural beauty.

-Being a Diver 4-

Health and Fitness

Diving is relaxing and you try to dive relaxed, but it is not always slow moving, even if you plan it to be, so you need to be in good health and reasonably fit. Strenuous activity can arise, including handling gear, currents, an emergency or unanticipated physical demand.

-Being a Diver 5-

Gas Narcosis

Gas narcosis is not thought to be harmful itself. The hazard is that it impairs the good judgement, clear thinking and timely responses you need to avoid and manage problems underwater.

Causes and Effects

Gas narcosis is thought to be caused by increased dissolved gasses in the body tissues slowing nerve impulses that travel in the brain and nervous system. It slows the communication between the brain and the body, and between different parts of the brain. Breathing air most drivers notice narcosis at approximately 30 meters/100 feet. Narcosis differs, however, from individual to individual and in the same diver from one to the next. Being tired, dehydrated or generally fatigued may make narcosis noticeable shallower, and some prescription and over-the-counter-drugs combine their effects with narcosis, making intoxication stronger. Cool water, darkness, and limited visibility seem to increase narcosis, though it may be that added stress simply makes the effects more noticeable. Diving experience seems to reduce narcosis - but pyscologists think you learn to compensate for it.

Avoiding and Managing Gas Narcosis

You avoid gas narcosis by not diving too deep. For the most part people, assuming no predisposing factors, gas narcosis isn't likely to be a concern diving as deep as 30 meters/100 feet. If you experience narcosis, immediately ascend to a shallower depth. Generally, narcosis fades quickly.

*Equipment*

-Equipment 1-

Primary Considerations

The three most important considerations when choosing scuba gear are sustainability, fit, and comfort.

Sustainability means the equipment is appropriate for you and the dive. If planning a dive in cool water, your short sleeve wet suit - perfectly suitable for tropical diving - may not be suitable.

Fit means the equipment is sized and adjusted for you. Many pieces of gear will not function, or function poorly, if they do not fit properly. Your PADI dive shop and instructor will help you learn to select gear that fits and adjust it.

Comfort means you can wear the item for an hour or more without a significant distraction due to its feel or configuration. Uncomfortable equipment that annoys you can take the fun out of a dive. It can sometimes distract you from safety issues. Be sure you gear fits and it's comfortable. To a point, a professional can tell if something fits, but you have to say whether it is or is not comfortable.

Secondary Considerations

Once your gear is suitable, comfortable and fits, there are four secondary considerations: cost and features, servicability, color and style, and accessories.

Cost and features - You can choose gear from a price range, with more comfortable features on high-end models.

Serviceability - Consider that some equipment requires annual or biannual professional servicing. Most dive operations service the brands they sell, so getting gear from your PADI dive shop typically covers this.

Color and Style - You can choose gear that looks good and is color coordinated.

Accessories - For each piece of equipment, your local PADI Professional can suggest related accessories. A professional at your dive shop may, for example, suggest retainers that hold equipment where it belongs, or an appropriate mask defog so you can see clearly. These contribute significantly to diving comfort and convenience. Do not neglect useful accessories.

-Equipment 2-

Exposure Suits

You wear an exposure suit on most dives for two main reasons: warmth, and or protection against accidental stings, abrasions, or cuts. There are three primary suit types: wet suits, dry suits and skin suits.

Wet Suits

Wet suits are the most common type of exposure suit. This is because they provide insulation and protection and also come in a wide variety of styles depending upon how much insulation you need. This makes them useful in water as cold as (10 degrees Celsius/50 degrees Fahrenheit) to as warm as (30 degrees Celsius/86 degrees Fahrenheit). Wet suits insulate you with foam neoprene (or other insulating materials), but you don't stay dry. Water seeps into the suit around your waist, ankles and neck and gets trapped. Your body heat quickly heats the trapped water, and the neoprene greatly slows heat loss. To work, though, the suit must trap water. If your suit is too loose, it will let water in too easily and not trap enough heat to provide insulation. If your suit is too tight, it will be uncomfortable and may restrict movement or breathing.

Dry Suits

Dry suits provide the most insulation. They have specific water-tight zippers and seals, and most types cover your entire body, except for your head and hands, which you protect with a wet suit hood and gloves. Dry suits used in the coldest waters (under polar ice) keep even your extremities warm. The air within the suit is heated by your body heat, and the suit's design prevents water from entering. This makes dry suits ideal for cold water diving. They extend your diving time in cold water and are essential for diving in polar regions, where water temperature is cooler than 10 degrees Celsius/50 degrees Fahrenheit. Dry suits create an air space around your body that provides insulation, but it also makes you more buoyant. You need to add air to the suit as you descend to prevent it from squeezing you, and release air as you ascend to prevent it from over-inflating. This requires some practice, but your PADI instructor will teach you how to do this during the Dry Suit Adventure Dive in the PADI Open Water Diver Course. Dry suits are more expensive than wet suits, and they require more maintenance. They also require more training to use properly, so you need to be a certified diver before you can take the Dry Suit Adventure Dive.

Skin Suits

Skin suits are thin, form-fitting suits made of synthetic materials like Lycra or nylon. They are typically worn over a swimsuit or under a wet suit to provide additional protection against cuts, scrapes, and stings. Skin suits are lightweight and don't provide insulation like wet or dry suits, but they do offer protection in environments where you might encounter sharp coral, rocks, or marine life. They are often used in warm water diving or as a base layer under a wet suit in cooler conditions. Skin suits are also popular for activities like snorkeling or freediving where thermal protection is not a concern but protection from the sun and marine life is desired.

-Equipment 3-

Surface Signalling Devices

Typically, you should have at-least two surface signalling devices with you on a dive. Of the two devices, you should have an audible device that allows you to attract attention with sound, and a visual device that makes you easier to see at a distance, especially amid waves. In an emergency you may use both - the audible signal to alert help from the boat or shore, and the visual signal so responders can see where you are. Divers commonly use the following devices, from which you'll choose two or more:

  • Whistles can normally attach to your BCD inflator
  • Low-pressure horns use air from your cylinder and are louder than whistles
  • Inflatable signal tubes are brightly colored plastic or fabric tubes that inflate at the surface. They stand roughly 1 meter/3 feet above the surface, helping make you visible in rough conditions.
  • Signal mirrors allow you to reflect sunlight to attract attention

-Equipment 4-

Mesh utility Bag

Besides your gear bag, you want on or more mesh utility bags. A mesh utility bag is a heavy-duty bag made from nylon mesh (so it drains) or other water-resistant synthetic.

  • Style - Some bags are all mesh with a drawstring closure. Others have wire or plastic frames that allow you to hold them with one hand open and locked closed
  • Material - Many mesh utility bags are entirely mesh. Others have an upper portion that is solid, so they last longer
  • Size - Mesh bags range from hand size to large enough to hold almost all your gear
  • Straps or Handles - Larger mesh bags, especially those intended for carrying gear, usually have straps or handles
